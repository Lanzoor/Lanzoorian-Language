Welcome to the Official Lanzoorian Language Guide!

Introduction to Lanzoorian

Lanzoorian (Lanzavel in Lanzoorian, literally meaning Lanzoor's) is a simplistic conlang created by Lanzoor.
Lanzoorian features the English alphabet as well as some of the Greek alphabet,
but it has a grammar similar to Korean and Japanese.
Much like the toki pona language, it features a simple vocabulary, but not too contextless at the same time.
Huge thanks to Discord users text_text_keke, 5merchant and _thecasualguy for testing, suggestions, general help, and inspections.
Feel free to DM the Discord user Lanzoor if you spot anything wrong (typo, grammar mistakes, general suggestions, or just reporting an issue)!

Please mind the fact that this language is still work in progress.
Things may get added as the development goes on, and change unexpectedly.

Many of you guys may struggle with the /ɸ/ sound for the alphabet Ff, since it is different from /f/ that we are all used to.
To make this pronunciation, first try and say hoo (/hu/). Your mouth must be rounded and close together, just like you are blowing air.
Now, force it into another vowel. If you want to say /ɸa/, you can practice by saying /hua/.
Next, combine the hu and a to the point where you are practically pronouncing the hu and a simultaneously. Now you will see yourself saying /ɸa/.
The sound /ç/ may be very confusing at first, but it is actually just a mash between a hard /h/ and a /j/.
So in English, if you were to say /ça/, you would just be practically saying "hya" simultaneously.

Syllables and Rules

All syllables in Lanzoorian may have indefinite syllable structure in Lanzoorian. (Considering that you are following the syllabary rules)
Here are the general rules / exceptions for making syllables;

[1]: Proper nouns and non-conjugated words must not end in Ðð, so switch it to a Θθ.
[2]: The alphabet before Yı must be a vowel.
[3]: You always use the lowercase alphabet, except for when starting proper nouns / indicating emphasis.
[4]: You can't just mash up two or more of the same vowel to make its sound long.
The same applies for consonants, even for syllabary structure boundaries. Omit unnecessary letters.
[5]: Diphthongs and etc. are NOT present in Lanzoorian, and any vowel clusters will be pronounced separately.
[6]: Consonants NEVER modify each other's pronunciation. Be careful with the alphabet Hh.
It can be used to ease / match the IPA, but does not actually modify and create a new sound.
[7]: This is the separator (′), which separates pronunciations no matter what. Make sure to use the separator if needed instead of the space bar.
The separator can also be used as a glottal stop in some contexts.
[8: The alphabet Ωω will be pronounced /u/ when used as a nucleus, and /w/ when used as an onset or a coda.

For non-pulmonic consonants, just use its closest pulmonic equivalent, converted using the the consonant conversions.
For example, /ɓ/ would be converted to just b.
For ejective non-pulmonic consonants (why would you convert these), just use its converted normal form (without the '
after it, converted following the consonant conversions) + '.
For clicks (I really don't know why you would convert these either), just use its closest representation of consonants.
(Something like tj for /ǀ/)
For affricates and combinations (coarticulated consonants) such as /t͡ʃ/ and /g͡b/,
just disassemble each letter (remove the tie bar), and convert teach letter using the consonant conversions.
For example, /t͡s/ would be converted to tc, and /t͡ɕ/ would be turned into ts. (following the conversions)

Counting

Counting in Lanzoorian is relatively simple, compared to other languages.
There are usually three ways to count in Lanzoorian.

Scattering (flokaca vel)

Scattering, or flokaca vel (meaning "disassembling" / "shattering" / "scattering") is a very common way to count in Lanzoorian.
Basically, how it works is that you scatter all the numbers in a large number, and pronounce each one, one-by-one.

Next, here are the demonstrative pronouns and demonstrative pronouns, used to either target anonymously or describe a noun.
But before that, you will need an explanation on the directional forms used in Lanzoorian.
Lanzoorian generally uses four directional forms. (not made up by me, they are used in English and Japanese)

  • Proximal - refers to the speaker's (in most cases, you reading this, the speaker) and the current direction.
    It can also be used to describe the thoughts of the speaker (again, you, the speaker).
    For example; "Look at this phone!", "Have you ever met that teacher before?" or "It is very hot this month."
  • Medial - refers to the listener (the opposite side)'s direction.
    Just like "this", it can also be used to describe the thoughts of the listener, from your perspective.
    For example; "What is that thing that you're holding?"
  • Distal - refers to directions that are far away from both sides.
    For example; "Look at what yon (old equivalent of distal) dog is doing over there!"
  • Interrogative - literally means which target the speaker or the listener is targeting to.
    For example; "What are you talking about?"

In Lanzoorian, you use the words for directional forms itselves for demonstrative pronouns
or combine a noun with the directional forms for demonstrative determiners.

Sentence Structures & Particles

Basic Particles

If you are not familiar with particles, you just need to know that particles are words that define sentence structure.
Of course, there are particles in English; we just don't call them that.

The Particle en

Structure

The structure of the particle en is as follows: 

[Subject] en [Object] / [Subject] en [Verb]

In this case, it literally means that [Subject] is [Object]. As you can see, the particle en means "is", and shares the structure with English. For example:

Examples

elω en Lanzor.

This sentence means "I is Lanzoor", which is translated into "I am Lanzoor" in English.
As you may have also guessed, the particle en can also be used to connect subjects into verbs. For example: 

elω en lito.

lito is the verb for "to eat". This sentence means "I is eat", which translates to "I am eating", or "I am in the state of eating" in English.
You will also learn how to add objects to this simple sentence soon.

The Particle vel

Structure

The structure of the particle vel is as follows: 

[Subject] vel [Object] / [Subject] vel / [Verb] vel / [Adj.] vel

If the particle vel is used with both a subject and an object, it means [Subject]'s [Object], or [Object] of [Subject]. For example:

Examples

elω vel fon

fon is the noun for communication, voice, sound, and phone. This sentence means "I's phone", which translates to "My phone" in English.
There are other uses of the particle vel as well; for instance, [Subject] vel would mean [Subject]'s possession. 

troca fon en alen vel?

This may be hard to understand, since it is our first questioning form. But there's nothing too complicated about it, the sentence structure is the same as the normal form. This sentence means "This phone is you's?", which is translated to "Is this phone yours?". Another one! When the particle vel is used before a verb, it can have multiple useful meanings. 

lito vel

This can have multiple meanings. The first meaning would literally be "the act of eating something", and then you can pair it with an adjective or something.
The second meaning would be "something to eat", or food. This means that the particle vel can also turn verbs into noun-forms.

The Particle el

Structure

The structure of the particle el is as follows: 

[1.] el [2.]

The particle el is the equivalent of "and" in English.

Examples

elω en flokaca el lito.
